A click-through tour of why sharing is slow today, the one simple idea that fixes it, and how the biggest apps in the world already do it this way.

The whole idea: instead of putting a label on every single file, we write down a few short facts — like "Sam can view this file" — and answer every question by reading those facts.

Why this hurts: a real folder isn't 24 things — it's thousands. Every share, every "make it private", every role change re-stamps the whole pile. There's even a background clean-up robot whose only job is fixing labels that fell out of sync. The new way writes one fact and never touches the files.

02 — The fix

Everything becomes one kind of "fact"

A fact is just a tiny sentence with three blanks. The shape never changes.

who / whatWing
relationshipis a member of
which thingthe Pharma room

Example 1 of 3 — a room membership

That's the trick. Room membership, "this file lives in that room", and "Sam can see this one file" are all the exact same shape of fact — they just go in the same notebook. To share, you add a fact. To un-share, you cross one out. Nothing gets stamped, copied, or cascaded.

04 — The worry that started this

"But people share single files all the time…"

Good instinct. In the old plan that was awkward. With facts, it's nothing.

Sharing one file with one person is just… one fact. Sam · can view · contract.pdf

No new room gets created. The file doesn't get yanked out of your folders. It's the same kind of fact as a room membership, living in the same notebook — just pointed at one file instead of a whole room. So the "single file → room → user" pile-up never happens: it's one row that says exactly what it means, and the access check already handles it (that was step 1 in the flowchart above).

06 — We're not inventing this

This is how the big apps already do it

The "write facts, follow the facts" approach has a name and a track record.

It's called relationship-based access control (ReBAC). Google wrote it up as “Zanzibar” and runs Drive, YouTube, Photos and Cloud on it. The open-source versions are OpenFGA (from Auth0/Okta) and SpiceDB. The golden rule in every one of them: store the facts, follow them at read time, cache the hot answers — never copy permissions onto every item. That copying is the exact thing we're trying to delete.

Google DriveYouTubeGitHubFigmaZanzibar (paper)OpenFGASpiceDB

But don't cargo-cult Google. Those engines are built for billions of files and worldwide consistency. We're a focused product, not Google.

So the right call for us: adopt the idea now — one "facts" table in our own database, with a small reader and a cache. Skip the separate authz service until permissions actually become a scaling headache. Shape the facts like the standard tuples so we could lift them into OpenFGA later, but don't pay for that complexity today. That's what teams our size (Notion, Linear, Figma early on) actually did.

07 — The honest snags

The tricky bits we still have to decide

The idea is clean. These few cases need a human call before we build.

snag 01

A folder shared two ways at once

The problemToday you can share a big folder with Anna and separately share a sub-folder with Bob. With one room per area, where does Bob's extra access go?
What we'd doWrite Bob a fact on the room (or, if it's really just one folder, a fact on that folder). Anything that widens who-can-see gets flagged in a preview for a human to approve.
snag 02

"Look but don't touch" goes away

The problemWe're dropping to two roles (Owner / Member), and Members can edit. View-only people would gain editing power.
What we'd doProduct call: accept it, or keep a temporary read-only fact for the few workspaces that truly need it. The "fact" model supports both — it's a decision, not a limitation.
snag 03

"Everyone at the company can see this"

The problemToday a folder can be visible to the whole company automatically. Rooms are opt-in, so what happens to "everyone"?
What we'd doOne company-wide room everyone is automatically a member of — which is, again, just facts. Leaning this way over killing org-wide visibility.
snag 04

Moving stuff between rooms

The problemWhen you move a folder into another room, the "lives in that room" facts for everything inside need updating — a smaller echo of the old problem.
What we'd doAccept it. Moves are rare; sharing is constant. Updating a "lives-in" fact on a move is far cheaper than re-stamping on every share.
snag 05

Not breaking anything during the switch

The problemWe can't flip a switch on live data and hope. If the new check is wrong, people lose access to their work.
What we'd doRun both side by side. New check answers; old system is the safety net. Only delete the old one once the new one provably gives the same answers.
snag 06

Keeping the strict-compliance rules

The problemSome workspaces have strict rules about who can be shared with. Those are load-bearing; dropping them is not an option.
What we'd doKeep every one, re-applied when we write a fact instead of when we stamp a folder. Same rules, new home.

08 — What we need to do

The plan to make it work

Build the new thing quietly, copy everyone over, prove it matches, then delete the old thing.

  1. Make a "facts" table. One place to store every fact: memberships, lives-in, and direct shares — all the same shape.build quietly
  2. Teach the app to read the facts. Add the new "follow the facts" check (the flowchart above), but keep the old system answering as backup.build quietly
  3. Give everyone a starter room. Each person gets a default personal room the moment their account is created.build quietly
  4. Build the room controls. Create / rename / archive a room, invite people, change roles — carrying over the compliance and limit rules.build quietly
  5. Make invite links work for rooms. Share-by-link, including the "join the company, then the room" path for outsiders.build quietly
  6. Write everyone's facts (the big migration). A careful, repeatable job that turns today's private / team / shared folders into facts. Runs as a preview first so we can eyeball anything that widens access.the switch
  7. Make "assign to a room" a proper move. With a clear "this becomes visible to N people" confirmation.the switch
  8. Reshape the sidebar and library around rooms. Group by room; one flat library with a room filter.the switch
  9. Use one check everywhere. Replace the duplicated front-end and back-end rules with the single "follow the facts" reader.the switch
  10. Throw away the stamping machine. Once the new check is proven, delete the old per-item labels, the clean-up robot, and the old share screens.clean up
  11. Sunset the old public share link. Keep old links working read-only, watch usage, then set a retirement date.clean up
★
Why this order: steps 1–5 ship invisibly with zero user impact. The migration (6) is the only risky moment, so it runs as a preview with the old system as a backup. Nothing old gets deleted (9–11) until the new check is proven correct in production.
