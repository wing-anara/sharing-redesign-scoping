A short, visual tour of why sharing gets slow, the one simple idea that fixes it, and how the biggest apps in the world already work this way.

The whole idea: instead of putting a label on every single file, write down a few short facts — like “Sam can view this file” — and answer every question by reading those facts.

Why this hurts: a real folder isn’t 24 things — it’s thousands. Every share, every “make it private,” every role change re-stamps the whole pile, and a background job has to keep fixing labels that drift out of sync. The new way writes one fact and never touches the files.

02 — The fix

Everything becomes one kind of “fact”

A fact is just a tiny sentence with three blanks. The shape never changes.

who / whatWing
relationshipis a member of
which thingthe Pharma room

Example 1 of 3 — a room membership

Room membership, “this file lives in that room,” and “Sam can see this one file” are all the exact same shape of fact — they just go in the same notebook. To share, you add a fact. To un-share, you cross one out. Nothing gets stamped, copied, or cascaded.

04 — Even single files

Sharing one file? Also just a fact.

No new room. No moving anything. One line that says exactly what it means.

One file, one person = one fact.  Sam · can view · contract.pdf

It’s the same kind of fact as a room membership, in the same notebook — just pointed at one file instead of a whole room. The access check already handles it (that was step 1 in the flowchart above), so quick one-off shares stay effortless.

06 — The track record

This is how the big apps already do it

The “write facts, follow the facts” approach has a name and a long history.

It’s called relationship-based access control (ReBAC). Google wrote it up as “Zanzibar” and runs Drive, YouTube, Photos and Cloud on it. The open-source versions are OpenFGA and SpiceDB.

Google DriveYouTubeGitHubFigmaZanzibar (paper)OpenFGASpiceDB

The golden rule in every one of them: store the facts, follow them when someone asks, and remember (cache) the popular answers — never copy permissions onto every item. That copying is exactly the slow, fragile part we set out to remove.

A plain-English explainer Write facts. Follow the facts.
